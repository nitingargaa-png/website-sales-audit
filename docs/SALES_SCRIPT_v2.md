# Sales Script — Pitching the Full System
## For Local Home Service Businesses
### v2.1 — Aligned with SKILL.md v12 (weighted health score, subject-line rules, no banned words)

---

## BEFORE THE CALL — PREP (5 minutes)

Run the website audit on their URL. Know these six things before you speak:

1. **Their #1 website problem** (broken site, no mobile, no reviews, etc.)
2. **What trade they're in** and the typical job value
3. **Roughly how many Google reviews they have** (check GBP quickly)
4. **One competitor fact** (from the audit's competitor look-up)
5. **Their weighted health score and band** (from Report B / Talking Points —
   e.g., "41/100 — 🔴 Needs Significant Work"). This sets your pitch tier:
   - Score ≤40 (🔴) → pitch Package 2 or Package 3 (rebuild + automation)
   - Score 41–65 (🟡) → pitch Package 1 + automation add-ons OR Package 2
   - Score 66+ (🟢) → automation-only pitch, no rebuild
6. **Whether they likely have gatekeepers** (larger HVAC/plumbing outfits often have
   a spouse or office manager answering — be ready with the gatekeeper script below)

Also note the **prospect type** from the audit:
- **Type A — Small/Independent** (1–5 trucks, Wix/Squarespace/WordPress DIY, Gmail,
  under 50 reviews): pitch the full system. Owner is the decision-maker.
- **Type B — Established/Enterprise** (6+ trucks, ServiceTitan/Housecall Pro,
  100+ reviews, financing widgets, membership plan): DO NOT pitch a website rebuild.
  Pitch the automation layer their existing systems don't provide — post-job
  follow-up, review velocity, missed-call text-back, Voice AI overflow.
  Decision-maker is the GM or Ops Manager, not the owner.

Do not wing this. Specificity is what separates you from every other person
who's cold-called them about their website.

## THE CALL — FULL SCRIPT

---

### GATEKEEPER SCRIPT (if spouse/office manager answers)

> "Hi, this is [Your Name] — I'm a web consultant and I left a voicemail for
> [Owner Name] earlier about their website. I found something they'd want to know
> about. Is [Owner Name] available?"

**If they ask what it's about:**
> "I did a quick review of their website and found a technical issue that's
> causing them to miss calls. It's a two-minute conversation — is there a
> better time I should try?"

**If they offer to take a message:**
> "That'd be great — just let them know [Your Name] called about the website.
> The best number to reach me is [your number]. It's a quick fix if they catch
> it now."

*The goal is not to pitch the gatekeeper. It's to get your name and a reason to
call through. Keep it short, specific, and confident.*

---

### OPENING (60 seconds)

> "Hi, is this [Owner Name]? Great — my name is [Your Name]. I'm a web consultant
> based in [Your City] and I specialize in working with local [trade] businesses.
> I took a look at your website earlier and I found a few things I thought you'd
> want to know about. Do you have two minutes?"

**If they say "is this a sales call?":**
> "I'm a local business owner in [City] too. I found something on your site that's
> costing you calls — and honestly, if I were in your shoes, I'd want to know.
> If I haven't shown you anything useful in 60 seconds, hang up. Fair?"

**[TWO-MINUTE BRIDGE — use this when the two minutes are up:]**
> "I know I said two minutes — we're right there. I have a specific fix for what
> I just showed you. Do you want me to take five more minutes to walk you through
> it, or should I just email you the full report?"

**If they say they're busy:**
> "Totally understand. Can I send you a quick summary of what I found? It'll take
> you 60 seconds to read and there's no obligation. What's the best email?"

---

### THE HOOK (30 seconds) — Lead with the #1 specific finding

*Pick the one that matches what you found. Be specific — name what you actually saw.
Add the job-value cost wherever you can.*

**If site has broken pages:**
> "When I clicked on your Contact page, it came back with a server error — meaning
> right now, some customers who try to reach you are hitting a dead end and calling
> someone else. For a [trade], that's potentially $500–$1,500 per missed emergency
> call. You'd have no way of knowing that's happening."

**If phone number is in an image:**
> "I pulled up your site on my phone and your phone number is inside an image —
> which means it can't be tapped to call directly. Someone in an emergency has to
> manually dial. On mobile, where most of your customers are searching from, that
> small friction costs you real calls. A typical [trade] job is worth what — $400,
> $600? A few of those a month add up fast."

**If no reviews showing on site:**
> "When I searched for [trade] in [City], the top results are showing 200, 400,
> 500 Google reviews. Your site doesn't show any — even if you have them on Google,
> no one sees them when they land on your site. That's a trust gap that costs you
> customers who are on the fence between you and the next result."

**If Gmail email:**
> "One small thing — your contact email is a Gmail address. It's a tiny detail but
> to a customer deciding between two [trade] businesses, it signals 'small operation.'
> Your competitor down the street using name@theirbusiness.com looks more established,
> and that affects who they call first."

**If site is not mobile-friendly:**
> "I pulled your site up on my phone and the text is so small you'd have to pinch
> to zoom just to read it. On mobile — which is where most of your customers are
> searching from — your site is harder to use than your competitors'. That costs
> you jobs from people who gave up and called someone easier to reach."

---

### DISCOVERY (2–3 minutes) — Ask before pitching

> "Before I show you everything I found, can I ask you a couple of quick questions?
> I want to make sure what I share actually applies to your situation."

Ask all of these — listen more than you talk:

**"Where does most of your business come from right now?"**
*(Referrals = website confirms trust. Google = website is the front line.
Word of mouth = they need reviews to back up the recommendation.)*

**"Roughly how many new customers do you bring in in a typical month?"**
*(This tells you how much a lead is worth to them.)*

**"When someone calls and you miss it — what happens?"**
*(Most will say "I call back when I can." This opens the missed call text-back conversation.)*

**"Do you have a way right now to follow up with someone who fills in a form on your site?"**
*(Almost all will say "I get an email and then call them." This opens the automation conversation.)*

**"How many Google reviews do you have — and when did you last get a new one?"**
*(Reveals the review gap. Most haven't thought about it actively.)*

---

**[TRIAL CLOSE — use this immediately after discovery, before the system pitch:]**
> "Based on what you just told me — missing calls when you're on a job, and
> following up manually when you can get to it — does that sound like something
> worth solving for your business?"

*Wait for a yes or a "yeah, it can be a problem." That's your green light to shift
to the system pitch. If they're noncommittal, stay in discovery — ask one more
question. Don't pitch to silence.*

---

### THE PROBLEM FRAME (1–2 minutes)

Connect what you found to what they just told you. Use their own words back to them.

> "So here's what's interesting. You said most of your customers come from referrals —
> which makes sense, you've been doing this for [X] years. But here's what I see
> happen all the time: someone gets your name from a friend, they Google you to
> confirm the recommendation, and they land on your site. Right now, your site
> [name the specific problem]. For some of those referrals, that's enough to make
> them hesitate and call someone else — and you'd never know it happened."

**Then add the cost frame:**
> "You said a typical job is worth around [their number]. If even two or three
> customers a month are hitting [the problem] and going elsewhere, that's
> [$X] a month leaving your business. That's more than a new website costs."

---

### THE SHIFT — From website to system (2 minutes)

This is where you separate yourself from every other web designer.

> "Here's what I want to share with you — because this is different from what
> most web designers talk about.
>
> A new website is a good start. But by itself, it doesn't do the whole job.
> The businesses in [City] that are consistently winning online aren't just
> showing up — they're following up faster than everyone else.
>
> Let me show you what I mean. When someone fills in a form on your website
> right now, what happens?"

*(They answer — usually "I get an email.")*

> "Right. And on average, how long before you get back to them?"

*(They answer — usually "later that day" or "when I get a chance.")*

> "Here's the thing — industry data on local service businesses shows that if
> you respond within the first 5 minutes, you're 8 times more likely to get that
> job than if you respond after an hour. The research comes out of lead response
> studies by Harvard Business Review and InsideSales — and if you've ever filled
> out a form and gotten called back two days later, you already know it's true.
> By the time most businesses call back, the customer has already booked someone
> else.
>
> What I build for my clients is a system where the moment someone fills in that
> form, they get a text back within 60 seconds. Not from you — from your number,
> automatically. That one thing alone changes the game."

---

**[PROACTIVE SOCIAL PROOF — deliver this immediately after the stat, before introducing features:]**
> "I'll give you a real example. A plumber in Mississauga I set this up for —
> first month, they captured 11 leads just from the missed call text-back alone.
> Calls that previously hit voicemail and moved on. Their Google reviews went from
> 14 to 38 in 90 days because every completed job automatically triggers a review
> request by text. That's not a special case — that's what the system does when
> it runs properly. I can show you their before and after if you want."

*Don't wait for them to push back to share proof. The ROI claim sounds abstract
without it. This story delivered proactively pre-empts skepticism before it forms.*

---

### INTRODUCE THE FEATURES — ONE BY ONE

Introduce each feature as a solution to what they told you. Never list them all at once.

> **HOW TO DESCRIBE THE AI RECEPTIONIST NUMBER (important — use this language):**
> Clients will ask "what about my existing number?" Use this framing:
>
> "You get a dedicated AI receptionist number. You can put it on your website,
> Google Business Profile, truck decals — wherever you advertise.
> Your existing number stays exactly as-is — nothing changes.
> If you want the AI to also catch calls to your main number when you're busy,
> we set up simple call forwarding — takes about 2 minutes on your phone.
> Most of my clients start with just the new number on the website,
> and they're already capturing leads they were missing."
>
> **Do NOT say:** "You keep your number and we route behind the scenes."
> This implies we control their existing number, which we don't by default.
> Forwarding is optional and client-configured — don't promise "seamless routing"
> without explaining what it actually involves.
>
> **Number porting** (if they insist on using their main number for the AI):
> "We can do that — it's a number port, takes 2–4 weeks for the carrier to process.
> There's a one-time setup fee of $250 for the porting work. Most clients find it
> easier to just put the new number on their site — same result, no wait."


**Missed call text-back** (if they said they miss calls):
> "You mentioned sometimes you miss calls when you're on a job. What if every time
> that happened, the person got a text within 60 seconds saying 'Sorry we missed
> you — what were you calling about?' Automatically. Most of them text back and
> stay in the conversation instead of calling the next result."

**Appointment booking** (if they have a quote process):
> "Instead of playing phone tag to schedule an estimate, customers can just pick
> a time from your site — like booking a restaurant. You'd get a notification,
> it goes in your calendar, and they get a reminder automatically. Saves you
> probably 30–45 minutes of back-and-forth a week."

**Review requests** (if they have few reviews):
> "After a job is done, your customers get a text — automatically — asking if
> they'd leave a Google review with a direct link. Most people are happy to do it
> but just never get around to it unless you make it easy. My clients typically
> go from a handful of reviews to 50, 80, 100 within a few months. That changes
> how you look in every search result."

**CRM pipeline** (if they mentioned losing track of leads):
> "Everything goes into one dashboard — every lead, every inquiry, every job.
> You can see at a glance: who's new, who needs a follow-up call, who's booked,
> who you just finished a job for. Nothing falls through the cracks."

**Chat widget** (after hours angle):
> "There's also a chat widget on the site that captures leads even when you're
> closed. Someone at 10pm who needs a plumber tomorrow morning — they type in
> their question, leave their number, and you wake up to a new lead. Without
> lifting a finger."

### HANDLE THE COMMON OBJECTIONS

**"I already have a website person / nephew / friend who handles it."**
> "That's fine — I'm not trying to replace them for the basic stuff. The platform
> I use includes lead automation, missed call text-back, and review systems that
> most web designers — even good ones — don't set up because it's a completely
> different skill set. It's less web design and more business operations. If your
> current person is already doing all of that, great — but most aren't. That's the
> gap I fill."

**"We tried a website before and it didn't bring in business."**
> "That's the most common thing I hear — and I'll tell you exactly why it happens.
> Most websites are built to look good. They're not built to follow up with leads,
> capture missed calls, or ask for reviews automatically. The website brings them
> to the door — the system is what closes them. A plumber I work with had the same
> experience before: nice website, not much happening. Once the follow-up was
> automated, it changed completely."

**"How much does this cost?"**
> "It depends on what you need. I have two main options. The Growth System — which
> is the full setup including the automations — is $2,497 to start and $297/month
> after that. Or there's a Foundation option that's just the website to start,
> at $997. Most clients go with the Growth System because that's where the real
> ROI is — most make it back in the first month or two from the extra calls alone.
> Which direction sounds more interesting to you?"

**"I need to think about it."**
> "Of course — no pressure at all. Can I ask what you'd want to think through?
> Is it the investment, or whether it would actually work for your business?
> Because if it's the second one, I can show you real numbers from a [same trade]
> I worked with and what changed for them in the first 90 days."

**"We're too busy right now."**
> "That's actually the best time to set this up — because when things slow down,
> you'll want the phone to keep ringing. In the GTA, [trade] volume can drop
> significantly in [slow season] — this gives you a system that keeps working
> even when you're not actively chasing it. And right now you might be too busy
> to follow up on every lead properly. That's exactly what the automation handles."

**"I don't want to be locked into a monthly fee / Can I just pay once and own it?"**
> "Completely fair concern. It's month-to-month — no contract. Most clients stay
> because the system keeps working and they don't want to manage it themselves.
> But if you ever want to cancel, you can. The $297/month covers your GHL account,
> monthly maintenance, and my eyes on the system making sure nothing breaks.
> Without that, you'd still have the website — you'd just be running the automations
> yourself, which takes a few hours a month."

**"We already pay for someone to handle our Google rankings / Google Ads."**
> "Good — this doesn't replace that. Those are bringing people to your site.
> What I'm building makes sure those people don't fall off when they get there.
> Right now you might be paying to send people somewhere that doesn't turn
> the visit into a call. This is the layer that closes the gap."

### THE CLOSE

**First — map their audit score to the right package before you present anything:**

| Audit Score / Band | Pitch Tier | Lead With |
|---|---|---|
| ≤40 (🔴 Needs Significant Work) | Package 2 or Package 3 minimum | Rebuild + full automation |
| 41–65 (🟡 Some Gaps) | Package 1 + automation add-ons OR Package 2 | Two-option close |
| 66+ (🟢 Strong Foundation) | Automation-only, no rebuild | Lead with post-job gaps |

If the prospect is **Type B** (established — ServiceTitan/Housecall Pro, 100+ reviews,
FSM detected), do NOT pitch a rebuild regardless of score. Pitch the automation layer
only — post-job follow-up, review velocity, missed-call text-back, Voice AI overflow.

**Then use this selector for close style:**

| Signal | Use |
|---|---|
| They were hesitant throughout, answered in short sentences, haven't asked questions | Option A (soft — mockup offer) |
| They engaged, asked about pricing or specifics, shared pain points openly | Option B (direct — Growth System) |
| Option B got a "maybe" or "let me think" | Option C (two-choice) as follow-up |

---

**Option A — Soft close (mockup offer — for hesitant prospects only):**
> "Here's what I'd suggest as a first step: let me put together a quick sketch
> of what your homepage could look like — takes me about an hour. No cost, no
> commitment. If you like the direction, we talk about moving forward. If not,
> you keep the ideas free of charge. Does that sound fair?"

---

**Option B — Direct close (primary — use when they've shown buying signals):**
> "Based on what you've told me, I'd recommend the Growth System. It solves
> [#1 problem they mentioned], sets up the automatic follow-up so you stop losing
> leads when you're on a job, and gets the booking calendar live so customers can
> schedule directly. Setup is $2,497 and then $297/month. I can have the first
> version ready for your review in about two weeks.
>
> One thing worth mentioning — I keep to one or two clients per trade per area
> so there's no conflict. I am currently talking to another [trade] in [City], so
> I'd let you know if that changes the timing.
>
> I'll put together your custom proposal tonight — what's the best email to send it to?"

---

**Option C — Two-choice close (fallback if Option B gets resistance):**
> "I have two options that fit where you are. Option one is the full Growth System —
> website plus all the automations — that's $2,497 to start and $297/month. Most
> people in your situation go with this because the ROI comes from the automations,
> not just the site. Option two is just the Foundation website at $997, and we add
> the automation layer in 30–60 days once you've seen the site working.
> Which one makes more sense for right now?"

## POST-CALL — IF THEY DIDN'T BUY TODAY

*(Authoritative source: docs/SKILL.md v12 FOLLOW-UP SEQUENCE block. This script
mirrors that sequence — if the two ever disagree, SKILL.md wins.)*

**Before starting the sequence — mandatory research pack (2 minutes):**
- Business name, trade, city, owner first name (if known from About page)
- #1 issue from Report A (exact wording)
- One quick win from Report B's "3 QUICK WINS" section (exact wording)
- Competitor stat from Phase 1 (review count gap or specific difference)
- Weighted health score and band from Phase 2 (e.g., "41/100 — 🔴")
- ONE live signal from last 30 days — check in this order, stop at first hit:
  1. Their Google Business Profile reviews → newest review (date + content)
  2. Their Facebook or Instagram → most recent post (topic + date)
  3. A local news item about their trade or city (heat wave, cold snap, storm, permit change)
- If NO live signal can be found: flag it and pause the sequence before Touch 3.
  Do not fabricate.

**Personalization tier — set once per prospect, applies to every touch:**
- TIER A (strongest): specific audit issue + named competitor stat + live signal → reference all 3
- TIER B (standard): specific audit issue + named competitor stat → reference both
- TIER C (minimum): specific audit issue only → acceptable only for Touches 1–2

**Subject line rules (apply to every touch):**
2–4 words, lowercase, no punctuation, looks like an internal reply — not a marketing
blast. Good: "site review", "quick note", "your homepage", "one thing", "saw this".
Bad: "Your Website Review — [Business Name]", "Following Up on Our Audit".

**Low-friction ask rules (apply to every touch):**
Always end with a question they can answer in one word. Never end with a calendar
link or "book a call" button. Good: "worth me sending the mockup?", "want the full
breakdown?", "useful?". Bad: "got 15 minutes for a call?", "book time here →".

---

**Touch 1 — Within 2 hours of the call:**
Subject: `site review` or `your homepage` (2–3 words, lowercase)
Structure: OBSERVATION → PROBLEM → ASK
Body: 3 sentences max.
  (1) OBSERVATION — name the #1 issue using exact Report A wording. Cite the
      specific page/element so they know you actually looked.
  (2) PROBLEM — one sentence on what it's costing them. Concrete, no hype.
  (3) ASK — low-friction question. "Worth me sending the quick mockup?"
Attach Report A only (SHORT version). Do NOT attach full report.

**Touch 2 — Day 3 (the quick-win nudge):**
Subject: `one thing` or `5 minute fix` (2–3 words, lowercase)
Structure: OBSERVATION → PROOF → ASK
> "Hey [First Name] — saw your [specific page/element] isn't set up as [specific fix].
> 5-minute fix in the page editor; usually pulls in a couple extra calls a month on
> its own. Want me to show you how?"

Under 4 lines. No mention of package, pricing, or "the system."

**Touch 3 — Day 7 (competitor gap — requires real Phase 1 data):**
Subject: `saw this` or `comparison` (2 words, lowercase)
Structure: TRIGGER → INSIGHT → ASK
Lead with the competitor signal. Be specific.
> "Hey [First Name] — checked how you stack up against the top result when I searched
> [trade] [city]. They show 147 Google reviews on their homepage; you show none, even
> though I know you've earned plenty. Different first impression entirely.
> Want a 90-second screenshot comparison?"

Never name the competitor in writing — describe them as "the top result for
[trade] [city]" instead. Never fabricate review counts or competitor details.
If no competitor data exists: SKIP this touch and move Touch 4 to Day 7.

**Touch 4 — Day 14 (story bridge tied to the live signal):**
Subject: `thought of you` or `this week` (2–3 words, lowercase)
Structure: STORY → BRIDGE → ASK
> "Hey [First Name] — with [live signal — heat wave / cold snap / storm / permit change],
> [trade] phones are ringing all week. A shop I worked with in [city-adjacent area]
> went from 3 missed calls a day to 0 after we set up the text-back. Thought of you.
> Worth a quick look?"

Modest, real numbers only. No specific client name without permission.

**Touch 5 — Day 21 (break-up — final touch):**
Subject: `last note` or `signing off` (2 words, lowercase)
Structure: HONEST CLOSE — no ask
> "[First Name] — going to stop reaching out on this. If your phone's ringing plenty
> already, ignore me. If things ever slow down or you want a second look at the site,
> you have my number. Either way — good luck out there."

This is the LAST touch. Do not continue after Day 21.

**Stop rules:** Prospect replies → stop and move to live conversation. Books a call → stop.
Says "stop" / "not interested" → remove from list. Sequence completes → re-queue in 6 months.

**Iteration protocol (after 10 prospects run through the sequence):**
- If reply rate on Touch 1 < 15% → rework the Report A one-liner.
- If reply rate on Touch 2 < 10% → the quick-win isn't useful enough; pick a different type.
- If reply rate on Touch 3 < 8% → competitor data isn't compelling; try a different angle.
- If reply rate on Touch 5 break-up > 20% → move the break-up earlier (Day 14).

---

*Update this script as you learn what objections and closes work best in your market.
After every call, note: what question opened them up, what objection came up, which
close you used and how it went. Patterns will emerge after 10–15 calls.*

## PAYMENT TERMS — What to Say When They Ask

When a prospect says "yes" and asks how payment works:

> "Standard split: half upfront before I start, half when the site goes live and
> you can see everything working. Monthly retainer starts on go-live day, billed
> on the 1st each month going forward. I send a GHL invoice — you can put a card
> on file for auto-pay, or I can e-transfer for the first payment if that's easier."

**If they push back on the deposit:**
> "The upfront portion covers the setup work — GHL configuration, the automations,
> and building the site before launch. You don't pay the second half until you can
> see it live. So you're not paying for the whole thing on faith — only half, and
> the rest when it's done."

**If they ask about cancellation:**
> "It's a 30-day notice to cancel — no long-term contract. Most of my clients
> stay because they see the results, not because they're locked in."

**If they ask for a discount:**
> Do not discount the monthly retainer. For setup fee only:
> "I'm selective about the businesses I take on, and I do offer a founding client
> rate for the first one or two businesses in a new trade or city. In exchange,
> I'd ask for a brief testimonial and permission to share the results as a case
> study — that's the trade. Would that work for you?"

**Consistent payment terms across all packages:**
- Setup: 50% before work, 50% on go-live
- Monthly: auto-charge on the 1st
- Cancellation: 30-day written notice, setup fee non-refundable
